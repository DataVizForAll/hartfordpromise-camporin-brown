Introduction

Have you ever wondered about the challenges faced by students grappling with financial hurdles on their educational path? and what percentage of these students complete their associate's degree at 2-year colleges? The content question we aim to address is this: “At each 2-year college (with at least 5 students who started), what percentage of Hartford Promise students who there, successfully completed with an associates degree?” It's a question that carries significant implications for our community partners, who are: The Hartford Promise Organization. HP is committed to providing high-quality education for students in Hartford. Their goal is to reduce the impact of luck on success by ensuring that every Promise scholar has access to essential resources. It is important to acknowledge that this data will be looked at by The Hartford Promise and then used to get a better understanding of what institutions their students will flourish at as individuals and as an academic. It is our pleasure to be working with such a heartfelt organization like Hartford Promise, that cares so much about their students and only wants what is best for them. As we further delve deeper into the analysis of the data, it is vital to define the term “HP students” as students facing high costs and potential financial challenges during their educational journey. In our research, we have identified variation in the performance of students under Hartford Promise at each school, indicating that students thrive in distinct programs unique to their institutions.

Findings

Let’s begin with the big picture. We examined a total of 254 students who were a part of HP. While about half of those earned a 4-year bachelor’s degree, we focused on a subset of 108 students who enrolled in 2-year colleges, but we focused on 11 students who received an associates degree from the 2-year college. Analyzing the data provided by HP and compiled by US CollegeScorecard. The data shows that Manchester Community College showcases the highest number of graduates at 10, surpassing Capital Community College with 7 graduates and Tunxis Community College with 1. There is a noticeable variance between the Hartford Promise (HP) and aggregate graduation percentages for each institution, which implies differing success rates among specific students. Manchester Community College stands out as the highest graduation rate at 21%. However, it is worth noting that the sample size Hartford Promise has provided us only includes a total of eleven students.

Methods

Our data is drawn from a masked data file sourced from Hartford Promise. The data includes information on students from the 2016 and 2017 cohorts, meaning they began receiving tuition support from Hartford Promise in 2016 or 2017. Our data parameters required us to focus on the students in 2-year programs, a total of 108 students from the two cohorts. In order to ensure a usable sample size, we only included schools that met the minimum threshold requirement of 5 students, yielding us a study of three colleges. Using pivot tables and formulaic logic through google sheets, we were able to break down the broader dataset into the information required under our parameters. As discussed prior, low sample size is a major restriction of the data. Traditionally, a minimum of 30 values is required to deem a sample representative. Despite the small sample size, this data is aimed to provide insight into what the true trends are within Hartford Promise students at 2-year programs. In order to optimize the data, similar analyses must be undertaken with future cohorts in order to ascertain the true nature of the observable trends.
